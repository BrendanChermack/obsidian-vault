## Investing:
- Long term
- less active monitoring
- focus on larger time frame price movements
- less concerned with short term fluctuations
- focus on FUNDAMENTAL ANALYSIS

## Trading:
- short term
- active monitoring
- focus on smaller time frame price movements
	- holding for mins, days, weeks not long term
- more concerned with short term fluctuations and less concerned with long term
- focus on TECHNICAL ANALYSIS
- day trading

## Fundamental analysis:
- Not used here
- long term usage
- find intrinsic value of the stock 
	- how much they are making or losing

## Technical analysis:
- statistical methods to find patterns to predict future price fluctuations
- market psychology researching into supply and demand for stock based trading vol, price studies, and patterns
- not concerned with financial positions of a company
	- short term movements
- all data gather through charts based on past or current trends
	- can be seen through charts

## Bid vs Ask and spread I:
- **Bid**
	- price you will pay to sell a stock to a buyer
		- lowest price a buyer is willing to pay for a stock
	- if i were to sell a stock it goes to a stock and that is the lowest price they are willing to buy
	- ex buy 10 shares on the ask and sell right away sell goes to the bid
- **Ask**
	- Price you will pay to buy a stock from a seller
		- highest price a seller is willing to sell their stock
- **Spread**
	- the difference between the lowest ASK and highest Bid
		- **ASK-BID = SPREAD**
- Market order vs limit order
	- market order:
		- simple and quickest way to fulfil an order (buy or sell)
			- purchasing stocks
		- when buying
			- your order will get executed immediately at the current ask price (highest price a seller is willing to sell a stock)
		- when selling
			- your order gets executed immediately at the current BID price (the lowest price a buyer is will to pay for the stock)
		- Doesn't guarantee the cheapest price
		- Avoid using on securities that have low volume (low liquidity)
			- securities: stocks, bonds, etc. investment stuff
		- Can use on securities that are highly liquid
			- recommended
	- Limit orders: (fishing order)
		- gives you control over entry and exit price
		- lets you set the max and min price that you are will to buy or sell
			- ex. if you want to buy at a stock at 20 dollars, you can set a limit order to buy at $20 and you will not pay over exactly $20 and you may get filled for even cheaper than $20
		- execution may not happen
			- someone may not want to buy at $20
			- not being able to sell? since there is no buyer that wants that price?
		- can be used on stocks with low liquidity 
			- wont lose with wide spreads on the bid/ask
## stop loss orders:
- SELL order that will only execute if the price of the stock reaches the target price or lower
- designed to limit you lose on a position
- sets a price trigger that automatically sells once a set price is reached
- protection from losses

	- don't have to be looking at it

## Bid vs Ask II:
- Bid:
	- Max price the buyer is willing to pay
- Ask 
	- min price the seller is willing to receive
- Users:
	- Sellers use the bid rate
	- buyers use the ask rate
- Ex. 
	- There are 3 traders Austin, James and Brandon. Austin and James currently own a stock and Brandon is looking to buy that stock
	- Austin is willing to sell his share at $104.50 and Brandon is willing to buy at that price or better (meaning 104.50 or less). This will happen since Austin is selling on the ASK and Brandon is willing to buy for that ASKING price of $104.50
	- Austin now has no shares left and thinks the stock will go up today. So he puts a LIMIT order to BUY at the BID(max price the buyer is willing to pay) price. Austin will buy his share from someone who is selling the stock. James who is looking to sell will sell to Austin on the bid
